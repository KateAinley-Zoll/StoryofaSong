Astronomy

Playlist and Listening Guide

 

Playlist

  1. James Arthur. "Falling Like the Stars" from You. Columbia Records: 2019

 

  1. Taylor Swift. "The Archer" from Lover. Republic Records: 2019

 

  1. Gracie Abrams. "I Miss You, I'm Sorry" from Minor. Interscope Records:  2020

 

  1. Lorde. "Supercut" from Melodrama. Republic Records: 2017

 

  1. Olivia Rodrigo. "Stranger" from Guts. Geffen Records: 2024

 

  1. Cavetown. "Meteor Shower" from Cavetown. Cavetown: 2015

 

  1. Conan Gray. "Astronomy" from Superache. Republic Records: 2021

















Listening Guide

 

Love is often idealized, with starry-eyed promises of forever and a happily ever after where they run into the night, disappearing into a galaxy of perfection and romance. But what happens when love fades, not automatically, but over time? Astronomy by Conan Gray explores this by using a cosmic metaphor to create imagery and illustrate emotional distance between two people. This playlist creates a story of a relationship, and how the idealization of love slowly fades as time goes on and through the experience of love itself. They break the illusion of love's perfection and instead show the raw, fragile, and complicated parts of relationships that are often overlooked by artists through dramatic love confessions and chaotic breakups. In contrast, these songs highlight the quieter pain of emotional distance, the soft demise of feelings that once felt unbreakable, and the slow realization that perfection was never truly attainable. Each track builds on the idea that love often leaves one person still reaching for something that is slowly slipping away. As the playlist progresses, the songs create a tension between the yearning for something that once felt endless and the slow awareness that love, like the stars themselves, is often fleeting. The cosmic imagery continues to resonate, but now, it’s filled with a quiet sadness, reflecting the emotional journey of understanding love’s short-livedness. Themes of realization and acknowledgement of the denied bridge slowly grow in this playlist just as a human would when they figure out what had happened. It's a reflection on how idealization and reality collide, leaving us with the bittersweet realization that love, no matter how destined, is ultimately fragile and imperfect. These tracks push back against the fantasy constructed by pop culture, offering instead a portrait of love’s fragility and fleeting beauty.

With romcoms and love stories shared through music and books, love is often seen as an unrealistic concept, twisted by societal standards to be what we “want it” to be. But perfection is impossible, especially in a relationship, yet many people end up learning this the hard way. In “Falling Like the Stars”, James Arthur uses the same metaphors as “Astronomy” but idealizes love with caution. Arthur imagines love as “falling stars.” The love in this song is an all-consuming, unwavering force with heartfelt lyrics depicting a love destined in time. Using hyperboles like “I’ll never let go” and the cosmic metaphor of stars, it shows how he sees his love as unshakeable. Falling stars being meteorite pieces waiting to burn up in the atmosphere emphasizes the dramatized love Arthur sings of, saying that the love is so big it may kill them. This song is a contrast to Gray’s song, highlighting the slow disillusionment even more. To Arthur, everything is perfect, he even sings about the future with this love interest, planning their days together with children and a house. This idealization creates standards of love for the audience listening. If they haven’t experienced love themselves, this may be the only exposure they have, creating a narrative that is simply not true. This conflicting idea of love juxtaposes “Astronomy” perfectly, highlighting the painful moment when promises (such as never letting go) begin to fade, underscoring the divide between romantic idealism and reality. Arthur’s idealism sets the emotional foundation for the playlist, marking the beginning of a love story before disillusionment sets in.

Before distance is created, doubt is introduced into the relationship. Taylor Swift's song “The Archer” highlights this with the metaphor of an archer, someone who is both a fighter and a target. Both “Astronomy” and “The Archer” use metaphors to describe the difficulties in relationships that are often overlooked. With Swift stating “I’ve been the archer, I’ve been the prey,” it shows her struggle with self-doubt and how she both attacks herself and feels vulnerable to others. Doubts in relationships are very common, but not as commonly explored in music. This aligns with “Astronomy”, where Gray describes love fading through cosmic metaphors such as “We connected like constellations” and “We can't keep holding onto nothing,” showing how distance grows between people regardless of how intertwined they once felt. Both songs use an internal perspective and echoing synth, which heightens the feeling of isolation, a major theme in both distance and doubt. Swift’s track signals the creeping in of uncertainty, the first emotional fracture.

The moment that a bridge is realized, it's shocking and devastating. Gracie Abrams explores this idea in “I Miss You, I’m Sorry.” She reflects on her emotions towards realizing that her once “perfect relationship” had concluded. In her lyrics, she realizes that she had idealized love and that idea had broken her more, after she realized the idealization was unrealistic. In the chorus she states, “You said ‘forever’ and I almost bought it,” an idea reflected in Arthur’s earlier song about how promises often lead to disappointment when in an idealized mindset. This is a direct contrast to “Falling Like the Stars”, where he stated, “I’ll never let you go.” It's almost as if Abrams is responding to Arthur, emphasizing how promises create unrealistic expectations. Abrams' confessional style gives a rawness to this song, similar to “Astronomy.” Her diary-like quality underscores the difficulty of letting go. Whispered and echoed vocals along with gentle instruments create an atmosphere of quiet devastation, much like what distance brings. Abrams states, “I gave you the world, but you just wanted space,” which highlights the experience of love slipping away with no way of helping it. Like “Astronomy”, this lyric emphasizes the heartbreak of watching love dissolve, even when there's a desire to hold on. Abrams’ song anchors the playlist in emotional clarity, the moment reality strikes.

This heartbreak takes time to overcome because, unlike a chaotic breakup, with distance it’s harder to find closure. There is constant self-blame and grasping for solutions by racking your mind with the memories of what went wrong and trying to deny the distance. In “Supercut” by Lorde, this exact idea is described. Lorde’s fast-paced and urgent delivery contrasts with the softness of “Astronomy”, yet both songs revolve around the replaying of memories and the longing for something that no longer exists. “In my head, I do everything right” highlights how people tend to replay only the best moments of love, ignoring the imperfections. This song is driven by the longing for an idealized version of love that never fully existed. A “supercut” is exciting, fast-paced, and full of grand gestures, it often ignores the more mundane or painful parts of real relationships. Unlike “Supercut”, which remains trapped in an illusion, “Astronomy” highlights the acceptance of faded love. Lorde’s track reflects the obsessive mind loop of loss, just before clarity sets in.

Olivia Rodrigo’s song “Stranger” continues the theme of watching love change into something unfamiliar. Her raw vocal delivery and sharp lyrics reflect the emotional weight of realizing someone you once knew has become a stranger. While “Astronomy” described the slow, inevitable drifting of two people, “Stranger” highlights the disorienting feeling of looking at someone you once loved and realizing they now feel foreign. Both songs emphasize how love can feel infinite and irreplaceable, only to be nothing over time. Rodrigo captures a sense of loss not just in the relationship, but in emotional connection. By stating “It’s like we never loved at all,” she shows her disbelief at how easily someone she loved could become a stranger. Rodrigo shows the reality that love doesn’t last forever. Her track deepens the playlist’s movement into sorrow and disconnection.

After the realization and doubt of the growing distance, sadness overflows one’s mind. In “Meteor Shower” by Cavetown, the lyrics describe a meteor shower, a metaphor for intense love, and how it has ended. The narrator is left in loneliness and longing. The idea that something so beautiful and looked up to, such as a meteor shower, disappears so quickly, can emphasize how transient love can be. With a quiet, acoustic arrangement and monotone vocal delivery, the song creates stillness and reflects loneliness. “But when I’m alone, it's like I’m in space” evokes the isolation felt after a connection has faded. Both “Meteor Shower” and “Astronomy” use cosmic metaphors to explore the complexities of love, but they approach it from different angles, Cavetown celebrates magical idealized love that turns into sadness, while Gray accepts the inevitable fade. Cavetown’s track lingers in mourning, a quiet echo before the final resolve.

The final step of this is acceptance. “Astronomy” by Conan Gray, the foundation of this playlist, is the final piece of emotional distance in relationships. The song’s themes of nostalgia and distance, using the vastness of space and stars as a metaphor for emotional detachment, emphasize the rawness of love and how idealization is unrealistic. The acceptance is seen through his lyrics such as “I guess I should’ve seen it coming, But I couldn't see beyond your smile.” This line speaks to the realization that despite understanding the idealization, the disconnection had already begun. The melancholy instrumentation creates a sense of quiet resignation. “I thought I'd see you in the stars, but you’re just like the ones I see in the sky,” shows how Gray’s dreams of a celestial love dissolve as he realizes the love he thought would last is as distant as the stars themselves. The track closes the arc not with bitterness, but acceptance.

This playlist tells the story of idealized love through the lens of cosmic imagery and a relationship’s steps, gradually breaking down the illusion and confronting the raw, messy reality. Each track builds on the last, moving from the dream-like expectations of love in “Falling Like the Stars” to the heartbreaking realization in “Stranger” and finally, acceptance in “Astronomy.” From the yearning echoes of many of these songs to the tender resignation in “Astronomy,” this playlist navigates the evolution of love from the celestial to the grounded, emphasizing the realization that love is, possibly, never truly written in the stars.
